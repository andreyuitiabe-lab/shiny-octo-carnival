# Seller Call Scripts — Word for Word

> Companion to `03-wholesale-process-playbook.md` §4. These are scripts you can
> read **verbatim** until they become natural. Three situations:
>
> - **Script A** — you call the owner cold (outbound).
> - **Script B** — the owner calls or texts YOU back (inbound — from a letter
>   or a missed call). Warmer: they already want something.
> - **Script C** — the offer & negotiation call ("trying to buy the lot").
>
> Golden rules for every call: **you are a buyer, not a salesman.** Calm, slow,
> friendly, a little casual. Ask, then **shut up and listen** — the seller
> should talk 70% of the time. Never quote a price on the spot (Script C is a
> separate, prepared call). Log every call in the Lead Manager before dialing
> the next one.

---

## What you need in front of you BEFORE any call

From Parcel Scout / county GIS (2 minutes of prep):

- Owner name(s) and how the deed is titled (one person? couple? estate?)
- APN, acreage, road it's on / nearest landmark
- Back taxes owed (if any) — powerful, but don't lead with it
- Rough guess of market value (even a loose one)
- The Lead Manager open, on that lead, ready to take notes

## SCRIPT A — Cold call (you call them)

### A1. Opening (first 15 seconds decide everything)

> "Hi, is this **[first name]**? … Hey [first name], my name is **[your
> name]** — I'm not a telemarketer, don't worry *(light laugh)*. I'm a local
> land buyer, and I was looking at the county map and saw you own that
> **[X-acre]** piece off **[road name]** in **[county]**. I was calling to see —
> would you be open to an offer on it?"

Then **stop talking.** Whatever they say next tells you which branch you're on:

| They say | You do |
|----------|--------|
| "How much?" | → **A4** (deflect price, gather facts first) |
| "Maybe / depends" | → **A2** (motivation questions) |
| "Not interested." | → **A6** (soft close, plant seed, schedule follow-up) |
| "How did you get my number?" | "Public county records show who owns land, and I use a service to find contact info — I only call owners of parcels I'm genuinely interested in buying." Then repeat the question. |
| "Is this a scam?" | "Fair question — I get it. I'm a real buyer, I pay through a licensed title company, you never send me anything, and they don't release a dime or a deed until everything checks out. No obligation — I just wanted to ask." |
| Hostile / "take me off your list" | "No problem at all, sorry to bother you — have a good one." Mark 🚫 DNC. Move on. |

### A2. Motivation questions (the heart of the call)

Ask these slowly, one at a time. You're listening for **inherited, tired of
taxes, out of state, never used it, just want it gone.**

> - "How long have you owned it?"
> - "Oh nice — what did you originally get it for? Plans that never happened?"
> - "Have you been out to it recently?" *(out-of-state owners: almost never)*
> - "Are the taxes on it current, or has that gotten annoying?" *(gentle)*
> - "If you did sell it, what would you do with the money?" *(reveals urgency)*

**Mirror technique:** repeat their last few words as a question. "We inherited
it from my father…" → "From your father?" → they keep talking. The more they
talk, the more the deal builds itself.

### A3. Fact-gathering (do this on every warm call)

> "Let me grab a couple details so I can put together a real number for you:
> - Is it just you on the deed, or is anyone else on it with you — spouse,
>   siblings? *(EVERY owner must sign later — find out now.)*
> - Do you know if there's any mortgage or lien on it?
> - How do you get into the property — is it right off [road]?
> - Anything on it? Old structures, a well, anything?
> - And taxes — roughly current, or is there some back-owed?"

### A4. When they ask "How much will you give me?"

**Never answer with a number.** Two moves, in order:

**Move 1 — flip it back (find their anchor):**
> "Honestly it depends on a few things I still need to check. But let me ask
> you — do you have a number in mind that would make it worth selling?"

- If their number is **at or under ~50% of your MV guess** → you likely have a
  deal; go warm: "That's… actually in the range I might be able to work with.
  Let me verify a few things on my end and call you back with a firm offer."
- If their number is high → don't argue: "Okay — that's probably above where
  I'd be, since I buy at a discount for cash. But let me run my numbers and
  I'll call you either way, fair?"

**Move 2 — if they refuse to name a number:**
> "Totally fine. Here's how I work: I pay **cash**, I buy it **exactly as it
> sits**, there are **no realtor commissions**, and **I pay all the closing
> costs** — you literally just sign and get a check from the title company.
> Because of that, I buy at a discount to retail. Let me check a few things
> and call you **[specific day]** with a real written number. Sound fair?"

### A5. Closing the call (always end with a scheduled next step)

> "Great — so I'll do my homework and call you **Thursday around [time]** with
> an actual offer. If you think of anything about the property before then,
> this number reaches me directly. Thanks, [first name]."

Log in Lead Manager: motivation, their price (if any), facts from A3,
disposition **Interested**, follow-up date = the day you promised.

### A6. Soft close for "not interested"

> "No problem at all — I appreciate you being straight with me. If it's okay,
> I'll leave you my number, and if the taxes or the upkeep ever stop being
> worth it, I'm a serious buyer and I make it painless. Take care, [first name]."

Disposition **Not now**, follow-up in 90 days. A big share of deals come from
these calls, two or three touches later.

### A7. Voicemail (keep it under 20 seconds)

> "Hi [first name], this is [your name]. I'm a land buyer here in [county] —
> I'm interested in **buying your [X]-acre property off [road]**, cash, as-is.
> Give me a call back at **[number]** — again that's [number]. Thanks!"

## SCRIPT B — They contact YOU (letter response / callback)

Inbound is warmer — they took an action. But they usually open with one of two
things: **"I got your letter — is this real?"** or **"I got your offer of
$X — I'll take it / that's too low."**

### B1. Opening

> "Hi, this is [your name]! … Oh great, thanks for calling back. Yes — that
> was me. I buy land here in [county], and your parcel off [road] caught my
> eye. So you got my letter — what did you think?"

Then listen. Route to the same blocks as Script A: motivation (A2) → facts
(A3) → next step (A5). **Do not skip motivation and facts just because they
called you.** An inbound seller who's never asked *why they're selling* and
*who's on the deed* becomes a blown-up deal at contract time.

### B2. "I'll take the offer in your letter" (blind-offer acceptance)

Stay calm — do **not** just say "great, sold!" Blind offers were computed from
desk data; verify before you're committed:

> "That's great to hear. So here's the process: that number was based on
> county records, so let me just confirm a few details with you, then I'll
> send over a simple one-page agreement, and my **title company** handles
> everything from there — you don't pay a dime in fees."

Then run **A3 facts** + Checklist Stage A–B same day. If everything checks
out, contract at your letter price. If you find a problem (landlocked, wet,
liens), use **C5** (renegotiation) honestly.

> ⚠️ If a seller accepts *instantly and eagerly*, double-check title and
> taxes extra carefully — eager sellers sometimes know something you don't.
> That's what your inspection period is for.

### B3. "Your offer is insulting / way too low"

> "I hear you — and honestly, you might be right for a retail sale. My number
> is a **cash, as-is, I-pay-everything** number, so it's below what you might
> get listing it for a year with an agent. Out of curiosity — what number
> would work for you?"

Now you're negotiating (go to Script C logic). If their number is hopeless:
A6 soft close, 90-day follow-up. Angry today ≠ angry after next year's tax bill.

### B4. Text replies to your letter/text

Keep texts short, human, lowercase-casual, and aim to convert to a call:

> "hi [name], thanks for getting back to me! yes, still interested in your
> [X] acres on [road]. do you have 5 minutes for a quick call today or
> tomorrow? happy to answer everything — [your name]"

## SCRIPT C — The offer & negotiation call ("buying the lot")

This is a **separate, scheduled call** you make AFTER the Deal Analyzer gave
you MV and MAO, and Checklist A–B passed. You never negotiate unprepared.

### C0. Prep sheet (write these 3 numbers down before dialing)

```
MV      market value from comps                e.g. $30,000
MAO     your absolute ceiling (Deal Analyzer)  e.g. $12,000  ← NEVER cross
OPEN    your opening offer ≈ 70–80% of MAO     e.g. $9,000–9,500
```

Open below MAO so you have room to "give" — sellers need to feel they won
something. **MAO is math, not mood.** If the call heats up, the ceiling
doesn't move.

### C1. Frame the offer (justify BEFORE the number)

> "Hi [first name], it's [your name] — calling with that offer like I
> promised. So, I did my homework: I pulled what similar parcels have
> actually **sold** for around there, checked access, the flood maps, and the
> taxes *(mention the $[back taxes] if any)*. Here's my offer, and let me
> explain how I got there.
>
> I pay **cash**, I take it **exactly as-is**, there's **no commission**, I
> pay **all closing costs**, and we can close in about 30 days through a
> licensed title company. Factoring all that in — I can do **$[OPEN]**."

Then **silence.** Seriously — say the number and stop. The first person who
talks after the number usually loses ground.

### C2. Reading the response

| Response | Meaning | Your move |
|----------|---------|-----------|
| "Okay / that works" | Deal (maybe you opened high — fine, MAO protected you) | → **C6** lock it down TODAY |
| "Can you do any better?" | They'll take something close | One small bump: "I can stretch to $[+5–10%] but that's genuinely my number." |
| Counter above OPEN but ≤ MAO | Normal negotiation | Meet between OPEN and their number; concede in **shrinking steps** ($9,000 → $10,000 → $10,500 → "that's my ceiling") |
| Counter above MAO | No deal at their number | → **C4** hold the line |
| "I need to think / talk to my wife" | Legit (and: wife must sign anyway!) | "Of course — should the three of us do a quick call so I can answer her questions too? When works?" Set a date. |

### C3. Concession rules (this is the whole negotiation)

1. **Never bid against yourself.** No counter from them = no new number from you.
2. **Every concession gets something back:** "I can do $10,500 **if** we can
   sign this week." / "…**if** we close on my title company's timeline."
3. **Shrinking increments** signal you're at the end: $1,000 → $500 → $250.
4. **Use the back taxes as a bridge:** "Tell you what — keep the price at
   $[X], and **I'll take care of the $[Y] in back taxes** on top. You walk
   away clean." (Same money to you; feels like a gift to them.)
5. **Justify with facts, never insults.** "The comps I'm seeing sold at
   $[Z]/acre" — never "your land isn't worth that."

### C4. Holding the line above MAO

> "I understand — and at $[their number] you might find that buyer, honestly.
> I just can't get there and still make it work on my end. My best is
> **$[MAO or just under]**. If you change your mind, or if it doesn't sell at
> that price in a few months, my offer stands. Can I check back with you in a
> couple of months either way?"

Walking away politely IS the play. Some percentage call back in 2–8 weeks.
Follow-up date in the Lead Manager, disposition **Offer made**.

### C5. Renegotiating after diligence finds a problem

Only renegotiate for **real, provable** issues — it's a trust withdrawal:

> "[First name], quick update — the title company found **[a $3,000 tax lien /
> no recorded access / X]**. I still want to buy it, but that changes my
> costs. I can keep everything moving if we adjust to **$[new number]** —
> and the lien gets paid off at closing so it stops being your problem too.
> Does that work?"

### C6. They said yes — lock it down (same day!)

Verbal yes → paper within 24–48 hours. Deals evaporate in the gap.

> "Perfect — here's what happens next, and it's easy:
> 1. I'll email *(or mail)* you a **simple purchase agreement** today — it says
>    exactly what we agreed: **$[price]**, I pay all costs, as-is.
> 2. You sign it — electronically, or pen and mail, whatever's comfortable.
> 3. I send it to the **title company**. They're a neutral licensed third
>    party — they check the title, prepare the deed, and handle all the money.
>    You never send anything to me directly.
> 4. In about **30–45 days** they'll have you sign the deed — usually by
>    mail with a notary — and they send you your check or wire.
> Does email work for the agreement? And just to confirm — it's you and
> **[co-owner]** on the deed, so it'll need both signatures, right?"

Confirm: best email, all signers, any questions. Send the contract **today.**
Set a follow-up for 48h if unsigned: "Just checking you got it — any questions
I can answer?"

## Objection cheat-sheet (all scripts)

| Objection | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| "It's worth way more than that." | "For a retail sale with an agent and a year of waiting, maybe so. Mine is a cash, as-is, no-fee, 30-day number. Different product. What matters is: what's it worth to you to be done with it?" |
| "The county assessed it at $X." | "Assessed value is for taxes — it's often way off what parcels actually trade for. I go by what similar lots really **sold** for; I'm happy to share the comps I used." |
| "I want to think about it." | "Absolutely. So I don't pester you — what's the main thing you want to think through? Maybe I can answer it now." *(Surfaces the real objection.)* |
| "My neighbor/son might want it." | "That'd be a great outcome for you — have you asked them? … Tell you what: my offer's good for [2 weeks]. Ask them, and if it doesn't come together, call me." |
| "How do I know you won't just steal my deed?" | "You never sign anything over to me directly — everything runs through a licensed title company in escrow. The deed only transfers when your money is sitting there waiting for you. You can call them yourself first, and I'd encourage it." |
| "Why is your offer so low?" | "Because of everything I'm taking on: I pay cash, buy as-is, cover the closing costs and [back taxes], and take the risk of reselling it. You're trading some price for zero hassle and a certain check." |
| "Another company offered me more." | "They might be a great fit — is it in writing? A lot of mailers quote high, then cut the price after 'inspection.' My number is my number. If theirs falls through, I'm here." |
| "Just send me something in writing." | "Happy to. What's the best mailing address / email? I'll get the offer out today, and I'll give you a quick call [day] to make sure it arrived." |

## After EVERY call — the 60-second log (Lead Manager)

1. Disposition (no answer / not now / interested / offer made / DNC / dead).
2. One line of human detail ("retiring to FL, hates the tax bill, wife Karen
   co-owns") — this line wins the follow-up call three months from now.
3. Their price anchor, if any.
4. Next follow-up date. **No lead leaves a call without a next date or a
   dead/DNC mark.**

## Practice drills

1. Read Script A out loud 10 times, then rewrite it in your own words —
   yours will beat mine because it sounds like you.
2. Role-play C1–C4 with a friend playing a stubborn seller who counters at
   2× your MAO. Practice saying the walk-away line comfortably.
3. Record yourself doing the A1 opener. Listen for speed — almost everyone
   talks 30% too fast on the phone. Slow is confident; fast is telemarketer.
4. Memorize exactly two numbers before any offer call: **OPEN** and **MAO**.
   If you can't recall MAO instantly, you're not ready to dial.
