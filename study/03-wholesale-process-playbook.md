# Land Wholesale Process — Step-by-Step Playbook

> This is the **deep dive on the wholesale (assignment) exit** — the lowest-cash,
> lowest-risk way to do your first deals. `01-operator-playbook.md` gives you the
> whole business at altitude; this playbook walks the wholesale deal itself,
> phone call to paycheck. Keep `02-due-diligence-checklist.md` open the whole way.

---

## 0. What wholesaling actually is (30 seconds)

You sign a **purchase agreement** with a landowner at a deep discount. That
contract gives you the *right* to buy. You then **assign** (sell) that right to
an end buyer — usually an investor — for a fee. The buyer closes with the
seller; the title company pays you your **assignment fee** out of the closing.

- You never own the land.
- You never bring the purchase money.
- Your only real money at risk is a small **earnest deposit** (often $10–$100
  on cheap land) and your marketing spend.
- Your product is not land — it's a **deal**. Investors pay you for finding and
  packaging a below-market parcel they didn't have to hunt for.

**The math of every wholesale deal:**

```
Market Value (MV)        what similar parcels actually SELL for
Your contract price      what the seller agreed to (target: 25–50% of MV)
Buyer's price            what your investor pays (they want ~60–75% of MV)
Assignment fee           buyer's price − your contract price  ← your paycheck
```

If there's no gap between "price an investor happily pays" and "price the
seller happily accepts," there is no deal. You manufacture that gap with
volume marketing and disciplined offers — never with hype.

## 1. The wholesale pipeline at a glance

| Phase | What happens | Money out | Time | Tool |
|-------|-------------|-----------|------|------|
| **1. Market & list** | Pick county, pull owner list, skip trace | Skip tracing (~$0.10–0.20/record) | Week 1 | County Scanner → Parcel Scout → Satellite Scout |
| **2. Outreach** | Mail / call / text owners at volume | Postage or dialer | Ongoing | Lead Manager (Call Mode) |
| **3. Seller conversation** | Qualify motivation, gather facts | Free | 15–30 min/lead | Lead Manager (script + log) |
| **4. Underwrite** | Desktop diligence + comps + MAO | Free | 1–2 hrs/deal | Deal Analyzer + Checklist stages A–C |
| **5. Contract** | Verbal agreement → signed purchase agreement | ~$10–100 earnest | Days | Attorney/title template |
| **6. Deep diligence** | Title search inside your inspection period | ~$100–500 | 1–3 weeks | Checklist stage D |
| **7. Dispo (find the buyer)** | Market the deal, collect buyer deposit | Free–cheap | 1–4 weeks | Buyer list + land sites |
| **8. Assign & close** | Assignment agreement → title company closes | Free (fees come out of closing) | 2–4 weeks | Title company |
| **9. Debrief & repeat** | Log numbers, feed learnings back | Free | 1 hr | Lead Manager + PROJECT-LOG |

The whole cycle for one deal typically runs **30–60 days** from first seller
contact to paycheck. You run many leads through it in parallel — the pipeline
is the business.

## 2. Phase 1 — Market & list (covered elsewhere, quick recap)

This playbook assumes you've done the hunting workflow:

1. **County Scanner** → pick a starter market (liquid, affordable, real deal flow).
2. **Parcel Scout** → pull vacant parcels, filter to *individuals, out-of-state*,
   sort by Lead score.
3. **Satellite Scout** → eyeball the top leads; keep the good ones.
4. **Skip trace** the kept CSV → phone numbers.
5. **Import into Lead Manager** → your call queue exists.

One extra rule for wholesaling specifically: **favor parcels investors already
buy** in that county. Check LandWatch/Zillow sold history — if 5–20 acre
recreational lots move there, target 5–20 acre recreational lots. Your buyer
pool defines your product; don't contract weird parcels no one exits.

---

## 3. Phase 2 — Outreach (the volume engine)

Three channels, in order of scale-per-dollar:

- **Blind offer letters (mail)** — a real dollar offer printed in the letter,
  at ~25–40% of your estimated MV (the Deal Analyzer's Blind Offer number).
  Pros: pre-negotiates price, responses are pre-qualified. Cons: postage cost,
  you can insult the occasional 1-acre premium lot — accept it, it's a volume game.
- **Cold calling** — fastest feedback, free-ish, and where the Lead Manager's
  Call Mode lives. Expect mostly no-answers; the game is dials per hour and
  honest dispositions.
- **Texting** — good for follow-up; watch compliance (see §10).

**Response reality check:** 1–5% of mail responds; most responders say no or
are unqualified; a common funnel is **1,000 letters → 20–50 responses →
3–8 real conversations → 1–2 contracts → ~1 closing.** Plan volume and
mindset accordingly. Every "no" is the system working.

**Follow-up is where the money is.** Most deals close on contact 2–5, not
contact 1. "Not right now" = set a 60–90 day follow-up in the Lead Manager.
Life events (tax bill arrives, divorce, estate settles) turn cold owners warm.

---

## 4. Phase 3 — The seller conversation

Your job on this call is **not to sell** — it's to *diagnose motivation* and
*collect facts*. You're a calm, low-pressure buyer doing them a favor by making
an as-is, no-fees, cash offer on land they forgot they owned.

### The intake script skeleton

1. **Open** — "Hi, I'm looking to buy some land in [County] and I saw you own
   the parcel on [Road / near landmark]. Would you consider an offer on it?"
2. **Motivation (listen, don't pitch)** — "How long have you owned it? What
   were your plans for it?" Listen for: inherited it, tired of taxes, lives
   out of state, never visited, estate cleanup. That's your deal.
3. **Facts** — confirm: APN, acreage, access ("how do you get to it?"),
   anything on it, back taxes current?, any co-owners (spouse, siblings,
   heirs — **everyone on the deed must sign**), any mortgage or liens they
   know of.
4. **Price** — "Do you have a number in mind?" If they name one first, you've
   learned their anchor. If they won't: "I buy at a discount because I pay
   cash, as-is, no commissions, and I cover closing costs — I'll run the
   numbers and call you back with a real offer." Never quote a price before
   the Deal Analyzer says what MAO is.
5. **Close the call** — set a specific next step ("I'll call Thursday with an
   offer"). Log everything in the Lead Manager.

### Motivation is the whole ballgame

A seller at 80% of market value is not a wholesale deal no matter how nice the
parcel is. The deals come from **discount-tolerant situations**: inherited/
unwanted, tax-burdened, out-of-state, "just want it gone." If motivation is
weak, disposition the lead as follow-up and move on — do not talk yourself
into thin deals.

## 5. Phase 4 — Underwrite (before you quote any price)

Run the parcel through the machine you already have:

1. **Checklist Stage A–B** (free desktop checks) — ownership, taxes, access,
   flood, wetlands, slope, zoning. Any KILL gate → politely pass, log why.
2. **Deal Analyzer** — 3–6 sold comps → market value → MAO.
3. **Wholesale-specific spread test.** Work *backwards from the buyer*:

```
Investor's likely purchase price   ≈ 60–75% of MV   (they need their own margin)
− your minimum assignment fee      (set a floor — e.g. $2,000–5,000; below that
                                    the deal isn't worth your cycle time)
− back taxes you'll absorb / concessions
= the MOST you can offer the seller
```

If that number is below anything the seller will plausibly take, **pass**. The
#1 wholesaler failure is contracting at a price no investor will pay, then
begging for a buyer, then canceling on the seller. It burns your money, your
time, and your name in a small county.

**Sanity anchor:** your contract price should generally land at **25–50% of
MV**. Inside that band, an investor at 65–70% of MV leaves you a real fee.

---

## 6. Phase 5 — Get it under contract

Verbal yes → paper fast. Deals die in the gap.

### What the purchase agreement must contain

Get a **state-appropriate template from a local real estate attorney or the
title company you'll close with** (they often share one free — it makes them
the closer). Do not improvise contracts. Non-negotiable clauses:

- **Assignability** — buyer named as "*Your Name/LLC* **and/or assigns**," or
  an explicit assignment clause. No assignability = no wholesale.
- **Inspection / due-diligence period** — 14–30 days where you may cancel for
  any reason and recover your earnest deposit. This is your escape hatch; the
  title search and buyer hunt happen inside it.
- **Closing window** — 30–45 days gives you room to dispo. "On or before" date.
- **Earnest deposit** — small ($10–$100 is common on cheap land), held by the
  **title company**, never handed to the seller directly.
- **Seller pays nothing** — you cover closing costs (it's your selling point);
  price it into MAO.
- **Marketing consent (recommended)** — a line acknowledging you may market
  your contractual interest during the inspection period. Some states
  effectively require this framing (see §10).

### Signing mechanics

- **Every owner on the deed signs.** Heirs/estates: probate may be required
  before they can sell — ask the title company early; unsettled estates are a
  common deal-killer.
- E-signature (DocuSign etc.) is normal and fine.
- The moment it's signed: **open escrow** — send the contract to your title
  company, deposit earnest money, order the **title search** same week. Title
  problems need maximum runway.

---

## 7. Phase 6 — Deep diligence (inside your inspection period)

Now — and only now — run **Checklist Stage D** with real money:

- Title search: liens, mortgages, judgments, tax certificates.
- Easements, HOA/CC&Rs, mineral rights status.
- Re-verify access and taxes against what the seller claimed.

If something ugly surfaces: renegotiate ("title found a $3,000 lien — I can
still close if we adjust to X") or **cancel inside the window** and get your
deposit back. Never let the inspection deadline pass on a deal you're unsure
about — calendar it the day you sign.

---

## 8. Phase 7 — Dispo: find your buyer

You have 14–30 days. Two motions, run in parallel:

### A. Build/work the buyer list (the compounding asset)

- **Cash-buyer mining:** county recorder data for recent *cash* land purchases
  in your county — those buyers are active *right now*. Parcel Scout data +
  recorder cross-reference gets you names; skip trace gets you phones.
- **Where investors hang out:** local REIA groups, Facebook groups ("[State]
  land investors", "vacant land investing"), BiggerPockets, Craigslist "land
  wanted" ads, other land flippers (bigger operators buy wholesale deals as
  inventory — find them via their own mail/websites).
- Every inquiry on every deal goes into the buyer list with their criteria
  (county, acreage, price band, use). Deal #3 sells faster than deal #1
  because the list exists. Track buyers in the Lead Manager too — they're
  leads with a different tag.

### B. Market the deal publicly

- Post on **Facebook Marketplace + local groups, Craigslist, LandWatch/
  Land.com** (paid but targeted). Price it at your contract price + fee.
- **The listing package:** county + acreage + price, 3–5 satellite photos
  (Satellite Scout output with the boundary drawn is exactly this), access
  description, zoning/permitted use, comps supporting the value, map link.
  Sell the discount: "similar parcels sold at $X/acre; yours at $Y."
- Be truthful and precise — you are marketing **your contractual interest in
  the parcel**, not land you own. Say "under contract — assignable" to
  investors; never claim ownership (see §10).

### Qualifying the buyer

- Cash or hard money, closeable inside your window — ask directly.
- Take a **non-refundable buyer deposit** ($500–$1,000+) with the assignment
  agreement. A buyer with no skin vanishes the week of closing.
- First solid yes beats a maybe at a higher fee. Velocity > greed on early deals.

### If no buyer bites

That's the market grading your underwriting. Options, in order: lower your fee
(a $1,500 paycheck beats a canceled contract), renegotiate the seller price,
or cancel *inside the inspection window* — apologize, release, log the lesson
(usually: contracted too high, or the parcel type has no buyer pool there).

---

## 9. Phase 8 — Assign and close

1. **Assignment agreement** — short contract: you assign your purchase
   agreement to Buyer for $X assignment fee; deposit non-refundable; buyer
   steps into your shoes. (Attorney/title template, same as before.)
2. **Send everything to the title company** — purchase agreement + assignment.
   They handle deed prep, lien payoff, prorated taxes, and the money.
3. **Closing day** — seller signs the deed, buyer wires funds, and the
   settlement statement shows your assignment fee paid to you. Cheap land
   often closes by **mail-away** — nobody meets anybody.
4. **Fee transparency:** on the standard settlement statement the seller and
   buyer can both see your fee. If a large fee will blow up the deal, the
   alternative is a **double close** (you actually buy and resell the same
   day/week, using transactional funding or the buyer's funds) — costs a
   second set of closing fees, so it only makes sense on bigger spreads. Ask
   your title company; not all will do it.

**After the wire lands:** thank the seller (referrals are real), tag the buyer
with what they want next, and log the deal's full numbers.

## 10. Compliance guardrails (the short version — verify per state)

- **Wholesaling is state-regulated real estate activity.** Several states
  (e.g. Illinois, Oklahoma, South Carolina, Kansas, and a growing list) limit
  unlicensed wholesaling or cap deal count without a license. Before your
  first mailer, read your **target state's** current rules and have one
  conversation with a local real-estate attorney. Budget $200–500 for that
  conversation and the contract templates — cheapest insurance you'll buy.
- **Market the contract, not the property.** The consistent legal safe zone is
  selling *your contractual interest*, with disclosure that you're under
  contract, not the owner. Never advertise land "for sale by owner" when
  you're not the owner.
- **Calling/texting:** scrub against the National DNC registry before every
  campaign (the Lead Manager's 🚫 DNC flag is your *internal* list — you still
  need the federal scrub); TCPA restricts auto-dialers and texts.
- **Everyone on title signs. Earnest money goes to escrow.** Both bear repeating.
- Nothing in this file is legal advice.

## 11. Your numbers dashboard (track from deal #1)

| Metric | Formula | Healthy-ish early target |
|--------|---------|--------------------------|
| Response rate | responses ÷ mail sent | 1–5% |
| Contract rate | contracts ÷ real conversations | 10–25% |
| Dispo rate | assignments closed ÷ contracts | ≥ 60–70% (lower = you contract too high) |
| Avg assignment fee | total fees ÷ closings | set your own floor; many land deals run $2k–$10k |
| Cost per deal | all marketing spend ÷ closings | must be ≪ avg fee |
| Cycle time | contract date → wire date | 30–60 days |

The **dispo rate** is your honesty meter. If you keep contracting deals you
can't sell, your MAO discipline — not your buyer list — is the problem.

## 12. The first-deal walkthrough (putting it all together)

1. County Scanner → pick starter county. Parcel Scout + Satellite Scout →
   150 kept leads. Skip trace → import to Lead Manager.
2. Call 30/day in Call Mode. Week 2: an out-of-state heir with a 12-acre
   wooded parcel, taxes 2 years behind, "just want it gone."
3. Checklist A–B: road frontage ✓, no wetlands ✓, zoning rural-res ✓,
   $1,900 back taxes. Deal Analyzer: comps say MV ≈ $30k. Blind offer logic →
   you offer **$9,500**; seller counters $12k; you agree at **$11,000**
   (37% of MV — inside the band).
4. Attorney template signed with "and/or assigns," 21-day inspection, 45-day
   close, $100 earnest to the title company. Title search ordered day 1.
5. Listing package (Satellite Scout photos + comps) to Facebook groups,
   Craigslist, and your 9-person buyer list. Day 6: an investor offers
   **$17,500** cash. Assignment agreement + $1,000 non-refundable deposit.
6. Title comes back clean except the known taxes. Title company closes on
   day 34. Settlement statement: seller nets their money, taxes paid off,
   buyer owns land at 58% of MV, and you're wired **$6,500**.
7. Log the numbers. Mail the next 1,000 letters. That's the business.

## 13. Study drills

1. Rewrite the §4 intake script in your own words; read it out loud until it
   sounds like you. Put your version into the Lead Manager script slot.
2. Find 3 active land wholesalers' listings (Facebook/Craigslist) in any
   county. Reverse-engineer: what's their likely contract price and fee?
3. Take one Satellite Scout "keep" and build the full §8 listing package for
   it as practice — photos, comps, one-paragraph pitch.
4. Look up your target state's wholesaling rules (search "*[state]* wholesale
   real estate license law") and write a 5-line summary at the bottom of this
   file.
5. Find one title company in your target county that handles land/assignments.
   Call them. Ask: do you close assignments? double closes? what's your fee on
   a $15k lot? That call makes the whole thing real.
