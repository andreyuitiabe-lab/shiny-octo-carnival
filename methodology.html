Overview

The model answers three questions, in order:

EngineQuestionTool
1 · Land-Flip ScoreWhere do I hunt?county_scanner.py
3 · Parcel ScoutWhich parcels do I mail?parcel_scout.py
2 · Decay + Blind OfferHow much do I offer?deal-analyzer.html
Core principle: rank markets for the land-flip spread — cheap land + active builders + population growth (the path of growth) — not for how fast existing homes resell. Residential resale liquidity (MLI) is kept only as a secondary "is the market frozen?" check.

Engine 1Land-Flip Score — where to play

Ranks ~2,000 U.S. counties from free Census population & permits + Redfin market data. The headline targets the land-flip spread; residential metrics (MLI) are a secondary liquidity check.

Normalization: Z-score (not percentile or min-max)

Each metric m for county i is standardized against all counties:

z_i = (m_i − μ) / σ winsorized to [−3, +3]

Z-scores preserve the shape of the distribution and tolerate the extreme outliers common in real-estate data; percentile throws away magnitude, min-max is hostage to one outlier. Winsorizing at ±3σ stops a single freak county from dominating. A missing value scores 0 (the mean = neutral).

Headline — the Land-Flip Score (path of growth)

Land flipping profits on the spread in the path of growth — cheap raw land where development is pushing outward — not on existing homes reselling fast. So the headline is the three drivers of that spread:

ComponentWeightWhy
Population growth (4-yr)0.40in-migration = the demand engine
Builder activity (permits/1k)0.35active builders absorbing land
Cheap entry (median price)0.25margin needs a low basis
LandFlip_raw = 0.40·z(pop_growth) + 0.35·z(permits/1k) + 0.25·(−z_price)
Why not lead with liquidity? An earlier version made the residential MLI 60% of the headline — but that surfaced mature metros (e.g. Monroe Co NY: top MLI, yet −0.7% population growth) with little cheap land and no spread. Resale velocity is nearly the opposite of land-flip opportunity. The rebuilt headline ranks the classic land-flip markets first (Horry SC, Bastrop/Hays/Montgomery TX, Sumter/Flagler FL, Pinal AZ).

Secondary — the Market Liquidity Index (MLI)

Residential resale liquidity, kept as a secondary column — an "is this market frozen or transacting?" sanity check, not the land-flip signal. Z-scored:

VariableWeightDirection
Months of inventory0.40lower is better (−z)
Days on market0.40lower is better (−z)
Sale-to-list ratio0.20higher is better
MLI_raw = 0.40·(−z_months) + 0.40·(−z_dom) + 0.20·(z_sale_to_list)

Both composites are shown as a readable 0–100 percentile.

Third lens — Hidden Opportunity (escape the obvious)

The Land-Flip headline rewards the signals every investor uses, so its top is the most crowded geography. The Hidden lens finds real demand the crowd is missing:

Hidden = LandFlip_demand − Competition − ExitPenalty
  • Competition (proxy, 0–100) = market size/visibility ln(pop) + ln(homes_sold) — bigger, busier markets draw more investors.
  • ExitPenalty = max(0, 3 − permits/1k)·6 — penalizes thin builder activity so we don't surface markets with no buyer (the "Custer trap").

A ⭐ under-the-radar pick = demand ≥ 70, competition ≤ 40, real exit. In practice the "next ring out" from growing metros (Trousdale TN↔Nashville, Gilchrist FL↔Gainesville, Linn KS↔Kansas City) + overlooked rural growers — competition 1–15 vs the obvious top's 70–98.

Two honest limits: competition is proxied by market size (real competition — investor purchase share, mail volume — is paid data: ATTOM/PropStream). And next step (logged): a cleaner version is the metro-ring model — score small counties adjacent to a growing metro via free Census county-adjacency data.

Validation status — read carefully

The MLI (secondary) is validated as a liquidity-persistence signal. Walk-forward backtest (backtest.py): rank by MLI on past data only, then measure realized forward liquidity:

HorizonMLI → fwd months-supplyMLI → fwd DOMMLI → fwd price (contrast)
12 months−0.73−0.74−0.03
24 months−0.75−0.76+0.01

High MLI predicts faster-selling markets; the ~0 price correlation shows liquidity persists while appreciation mean-reverts.

The Land-Flip Score (headline) is NOT yet empirically validated. It is thesis-driven — it encodes how experienced land investors pick markets (cheap + builders + growth) — but it hasn't been measured against realized land outcomes (land STR/DOM), which needs land-specific sale data (the paid/scraped next step). Treat it as a well-reasoned prior, not a proven predictor, until validated against land transactions.

Engine 2Parcel pricing — how much to offer

2a · Acreage Decay Curve (valuation)

Land doesn't price linearly per acre — a big parcel is worth less per acre than a small one. Value follows a power law (b is negative, the "decay exponent"):

PPA = a · acres^b

We fit b from your comps by least squares on the log-log form (ln(PPA) = ln(a) + b·ln(acres)), needing ≥3 comps across ≥2 sizes; otherwise a default b = −0.35. b is clamped to [−0.8, 0]. Each comp is then re-priced to the subject's acreage before averaging:

PPA_subject(comp i) = PPA_i · (acres_subject / acres_i)^b EMV = mean_i( PPA_subject(comp i) ) · acres_subject
Worked example: comps of 2 ac ($10k/ac), 5 ac ($7k/ac) and 40 ac ($3k/ac) all converge to ≈ $3,975/ac for a 20-ac subject (EMV ≈ $79.5k) — vs a naive flat average of $6,667/ac that would over-value it by ~68%.

2b · Blind-Offer Matrix (the offer you mail)

Offer_blind = EMV · (1 − D)

D = base margin + situational modifiers, clamped to [25%, 40%]:

ComponentConditionΔ discount
Baseevery parcel+0.32
Acreage tierTier 1 (1–5 ac)−0.04
Tier 2 (5–10 ac)0.00
Tier 3 (10–20 ac)+0.02
Tier 4 (20+ ac)+0.05
Market velocityDOM < 45 days (hot)−0.04
DOM ≥ 120 days (slow)+0.03
Seller distresstaxes > 1 yr delinquent+0.05
D = clamp( 0.32 + tier + velocity + distress, 0.25, 0.40 ) Offer_blind = max( EMV·(1−D), floor_$/acre · acres )

Tier 1 gets a smaller discount (compete on small, liquid lots); Tier 4 a deeper one (more capital at risk). Hot markets compress the discount; distress widens it. A $/acre floor blocks absurd lowballs.

2c · Unifying the two offer philosophies

Blind offerwhat you mail (the opening, from the matrix above)
MAO (ceiling)resale − costs − target_profit — your walk-away max
Recommended openingmin(Offer_blind, MAO)

If Offer_blind > MAO, the margin is too thin (your opening already exceeds your ceiling) → verdict flags CAUTION to tighten costs/comps or walk. Killer diligence gates (access, topography, zoning, flood, wetlands) force KILL; clear gates + room between blind offer and MAO → GO.

Engine 3Parcel Scout — which parcels to mail (automation)

Pulls and pre-screens every vacant parcel in a county, free, from the NYS statewide parcel ArcGIS service (38 NY counties incl. top-MLI land markets: Genesee, Wayne, Livingston, Ontario, Steuben…). Vacant land is isolated by NYS property-class code 3xx.

Lead score (prioritization, not a buy signal)

Lead = 100 · ( 0.40·motivation + 0.25·size + 0.35·cheap )
  • motivation = 1.0 if owner mails from out of state, 0.6 if out of town, else 0. × 0.3 if the owner is a corporation (cell towers, ag LLCs, banks, governments are rarely motivated sellers).
  • size = 1.0 for the liquid 2–20 ac band, 0.5 for 20+ ac, 0.3 otherwise.
  • cheap = 1 − percentile(assessed $/acre). (Assessed value is a free proxy; real value comes from sold comps in the Deal Analyzer.)

Auto-diligence (free, on the top N leads)

Each top parcel's centroid lat/lon is queried against three free federal layers; any failure degrades to ? (gate = CHECK) rather than crashing.

State legal layer (compliance flag)

Wholesaling is regulated at the state level and the rules changed sharply in 2024–2025. Every county gets a legal-status badge from its state — a flag, not a score input (a great market in a restrictive state still shows its true score, just badged). Snapshot, mid-2026:

TierStatesMeaning
⛔ License req'dIL, OK, NC, KY, NEa real-estate license is effectively required

Not legal advice. These laws change fast and edge cases are contested — always confirm with a licensed attorney in the target state before operating. Implements the "exclusion zones" idea from Module 5 of the blueprint.

Assumptions & limitations

  • Residential proxy. The MLI uses residential liquidity as a stand-in for land liquidity (no free nationwide land-listing data). Validated as directional, not exact.
  • No free land comps. Valuation still needs comps entered by hand; Scout's assessed-value proxy is for ranking, not offers.
  • Parcel data is per-county and NY-only for now. National one-click parcel + skip-trace is the paid path (BatchData + PRYCD, ≈$1,200/mo).
  • Diligence is a desktop pre-screen. The overlays flag likely problems; they don't replace a title search, survey, or on-the-ground check.
  • Not advice. Educational tooling. Confirm title, access, and contracts with a title company or attorney before closing.
